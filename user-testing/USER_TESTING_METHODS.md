## User Testing

The approach taken to user testing was the combination of the System Usability Scale (SUS) and an open-ended questionnaire. The test participants were given scenarios aiming to cover the core system features involved in both the staff and resident experiences. After completing these tasks, the participants completed the survey and questionnaire. The SUS was chosen due to its standardized measurement through its scoring system and its reliability when working with smaller sample sizes~\cite{sus_context}, while the open-ended questions provided valuable insights from the user's thoughts and experiences while interacting the system.

### User Manual - Resident User Testing

**Residents Context**  
Residents visit Hounslow House on a daily basis to get help with various council services, enquiries, and support. Hounslow Council has introduced a new web application that allows residents to book an appointment or join a digital queue by filling out a form. Residents can also use the website to check in for their appointment or view their queue information.

#### The Scenarios

**Scenario 1**  
You need help from Hounslow Council regarding a homelessness issue and have been advised to visit Hounslow House. Before arriving, you discover that appointments can be booked online by completing a form.

You continue as a visitor and book an appointment through the website so you can speak to someone about your situation.

After booking, you receive a reference code for your appointment.

**Scenario 2**  
You need help from Hounslow Council regarding a council tax enquiry and have been advised to visit Hounslow House. You discover that you can join the digital queue to be seen today. This can be done by completing an online form.

You continue as a visitor and join the queue on the website, so you can speak to someone about your situation.

After joining the digital queue, you will receive a ticket number. Using the website, check your position in the queue.

**Scenario 3**  
You need help from Hounslow Council regarding a ? enquiry and have been advised to visit Hounslow House. You discover that you can join the digital queue to be seen today. This can be done by completing an online form.

You create an account and join the queue, so you can speak to someone about your situation.

After joining the digital queue, you will receive a QR code. Using the website, check your position in the queue.

**Scenario 4**  
You need help from Hounslow Council regarding child safety issues and have been advised to visit Hounslow House. Before arriving, you discover that you can join the digital queue to be seen today by completing an online form.

Continue as a visitor and join the queue so you can speak to someone about your situation.

After joining the queue, you receive a ticket number. Use the website to check your position in the queue.

After waiting for a while, you see that the estimated wait time is over 40 minutes. Since you have some time, you decide to step outside for a short walk.

About 15 minutes later, you return to Hounslow House and want to make sure your place in the queue is still handled correctly.

**Scenario 5**  
You need help from Hounslow Council regarding a homelessness issue and have been advised to visit Hounslow House. Before arriving, you discover that appointments can be booked online by completing a form.

You continue as a visitor and book an appointment through the website so you can speak to someone about your situation.

After booking, you receive a reference code for your appointment. Later, your plans change, and you can no longer attend. Use the website to cancel your appointment.

**Scenario 6**  
Your sister needs help from Hounslow Council regarding a homelessness issue, and you’ve been advised to visit Hounslow House. You find out that she can join the digital queue to be seen today by completing an online form.

Using the website, you continue as a visitor and join the queue on behalf of your sister so she can speak to someone about her situation.

After joining the digital queue, you receive a ticket number and give it to your sister. Since she speaks limited English, you advise her to switch the website to her preferred language. Then, using the website, she checks her current position in the queue.

### User Manual - Staff User Testing

**Staff Context**  
Staff at Hounslow House assist residents with a wide range of council services, enquiries, and support. Hounslow Council has introduced a new web application to help staff identify and record issues related to resident cases. The system also allows staff to manually adjust case positions in the queue and edit case details when necessary.

#### The Scenarios

**Scenario 1**  
It’s the start of your shift at Hounslow House. You log in to the system and check the dashboard, which shows the queues for each department, urgent cases, and residents who have stepped out.

While reviewing the cases from the Children’s Duty department, you notice one that requires urgent attention. You mark it as a priority and adjust its position in the queue so it will be handled sooner.

**Scenario 2**  
It’s the middle of your shift at Hounslow House. You log in to the system and glance at the dashboard for an overview of activity. Using the sidebar, you review all tickets currently in reception.

A resident arrives with a reference number for their appointment. You check them in using the system and confirm that the check-in is successful.

**Scenario 3**  
It’s the start of your shift at Hounslow House. As a member of the Homelessness department, you log in to the system and review the day’s activity. You review the current queue for your department, adding notes as needed and reviewing cases flagged as safeguarding issues. You also notice a new case that may have safety concerns and flag it for attention.

**Scenario 4**  
It’s the middle of your shift at Hounslow House, and you are working in the Council Tax department. You log in and review the dashboard to get an overview of the day’s activity. After seeing a resident, you return to your department’s queue and mark their case as seen.

**Scenario 5**  
It’s the start of your shift at Hounslow House. You log in to the system and glance at the dashboard for an overview of activity. Using the sidebar, you can review all cases. After meeting with a resident with a housing benefit enquiry and assisting them, their issue has now been fully resolved, and they no longer require further support. Update the system to reflect that this case is resolved.

### Survey Questions

First the survey asked the users to rate the SUS scale questions on a ranking from 1-5:

1. I think that I would like to use this system frequently.
2. I found the system unnecessarily complex.
3. I thought the system was easy to use.
4. I think that I would need the support of a technical person to be able to use this system.
5. I found the various functions in this system were well integrated.
6. I thought there was too much inconsistency in this system.
7. I would imagine that most people would learn to use this system very quickly.
8. I found the system very cumbersome to use.
9. I felt very confident using the system.
10. I needed to learn a lot of things before I could get going with this system.

Then the survey asked users the following questions for our own insight:

- Are you filling this form as a Resident user or a Staff user? (Choose: Resident or Staff)
- Were you able to complete all the tasks? (Yes or no)
- Which scenario(s) did you complete? (Multi-select from a list)
- OPTIONAL: Did you get stuck at any point when using the system? (Free text)
- OPTIONAL: Did you have any trouble using the system? (Free text)
- OPTIONAL: Do you have any feedback for this app? (Free text)

### Data collection & Results

The survey was sent as a Google Form, and the responses were collected.

### Analysis

A System Usability Scale score of 76.1 suggests that participants viewed the system positively in terms of usability. In practice, this places the prototype above the typical average reported for many digital systems and products, indicating that users generally found it straightforward and usable. Scores in this range are often associated with a good level of perceived usability, so the result suggests that the overall interaction design was effective for the participants who tested it.

Even so, the score should not be treated as evidence that the system is finished or without issues. The open-ended questionnaire remains important because it helps explain which parts of the experience worked well and which areas still caused confusion, hesitation, or friction. Taken together, the SUS result and the qualitative feedback suggest that the prototype provides a strong usability baseline while still leaving room for refinement in later iterations.
